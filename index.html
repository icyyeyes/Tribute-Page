Mother Teresa

Yesterday is gone. Tomorrow has not yet come. We have only today. Let us begin.

Mother Mary Teresa Bojaxhiu was an Albanian-Indian Roman Catholic Nun and missionary.

Biography

Mother Teresa was born on 26 August 1910 into a Kosovar Albanian family in Skopje, the capital of North Macedonia.
She left home in 1928 at age 18 to join Sisters of Loreto at Loreto Abbey in Rathfarnham, Ireland to leran English with the intent of becoming a missionary.
She arrived in India in 1929. Teresa took her first religious vows on 24 May 1931. She took her solemn vows on 14 May 1937.
On 13 March 1997 Teresa resigned as head of Missionaries of Charity, and she died on 5 September. She recieved a state funeral from the Indian Government in gratitude for her service for the poor of all religions in the country.

Recognition and Reception

India

Teresa was first recognised by Indian Government more then a third of a century earlier.
She recieved the Padma Shri Award in 1962 and the Jawahar Lal Nehru Award for International Understanding in 1969.
Teresa's official biography by Navin Chawla was published in 1992.

Elsewhere

Mother Teresa recieved Ramon Magsaysay Award for Peace and International Understanding, given for work in, South or East Asia in 1962.
Pope Paul VI gave her the inaugural Pope Jhon XXIII Peace Prize in 1971. She recieved the Pacem in Terris Award in 1976.
In 1979 she recieved the Nobel Peace Prize for work undertaken in the steuggle to overcome poverty and distress, which also consitutes a threat to peace.
In 1999 she headed Gallup's List for Most Widely Admired People of the 20th Century.

Nobel Peace Prize

In 1979, Mother Tersa recieved the Nobel Peace Prize "for work undertaken in the struggle to overcome the poverty and distress which also, constitutes a threat to peace.
When she recieved the prize she was asked, "What can we do to promote world peace?" She answered, " "Go home and love your family." Building on this theme in her Nobel Lecture, she said: "Around the world, not only the poor countries, but I foundthe poverty of the West so much more difficult to remove. When I pickup a person from the street, hungry, I gave him a plate of rice, a piece of bread, I have satisfied. I have removed that hunger. But the person that is shut out, that feels unwanted, unloved, terrified, the person that has been thrown out from society - that poverty is so hurtable and so much, and I find that very difficut.

Social and Political Views

Teresa singled out abortion as "the greatest destroyer of peace today. Because if a mother can kill her own child – what is left for me to kill you and you kill me – there is nothing between."
As the Fourth World Confrence on Women in Beijing, Teresa said: "Yet we can destroy this gift of motherhood, especially by the evil of abortion, but also by thinking that other things like jobs or positions are more important than loving."
